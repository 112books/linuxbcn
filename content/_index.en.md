## Most digital problems aren't technical

They're about structure.

You have a website you can't update because it depends on an agency that takes three weeks to reply.
Your Instagram has 2,000 followers but your domain expires next month.
You use big-tech tools because everyone does,
but your communications sit on servers you don't control and can't audit.
Your contact form sends data to an American server you've never heard of.

Often, the problem isn't that you lack technology.
It's that you have too much technology you don't understand,
don't control, and couldn't change even if you wanted to.

## Free software isn't ideology. It's practice.

When you use free tools, the code is public.
Someone has checked whether it does what it says it does.
Someone has verified it doesn't send your data to places you haven't authorised.

When you use closed tools, you have to trust.
And trust is expensive when what's at stake is your relationship with your audience,
your internal communications, or your clients' data.

We have seen cultural projects lose years of work
because a platform changed its terms overnight.
We have seen musicians without access to their own fan base
because it lived in an account they didn't control.
We have seen collectives paying indefinitely for tools they didn't need
but didn't know how to remove.

Digital sovereignty isn't a luxury. It's basic hygiene.
